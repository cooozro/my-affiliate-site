## Capacity: mAh labels vs usable watt-hours

**Milliamp-hours (mAh)** describe how much charge a pack stores at its internal cell voltage (typically 3.7V). **Watt-hours (Wh)** convert that into energy independent of voltage: `Wh ≈ mAh × 3.7 ÷ 1000`. A 10,000mAh pack is roughly **37Wh**; 20,000mAh is about **74Wh**.

Why Wh matters in summer travel:

- **Airline carry-on limits** often cap spare lithium batteries at 100Wh without airline approval; packs above 100Wh may be banned from cabin baggage on many carriers.
- **Phone cycles** depend on your phone’s battery size and conversion losses. A 37Wh bank might deliver one full charge to a modern flagship, not two — heat and fast-charge overhead reduce the headline mAh.
- **Marketing inflation** — Some brands quote “rated” mAh at 5V output, which inflates the sticker versus true cell capacity.

Rule of thumb for vacation days: match **Wh to daily drain**, not mAh alone. Heavy map use, camera, and hotspot sharing in heat can push a phone from 100% to 20% in half a day; plan Wh accordingly.

## Charging speed: USB Power Delivery and heat

**USB Power Delivery (USB-C PD)** negotiates voltage and current between the power bank, cable, and phone. A 30W PD port can charge many Android flagships and iPhones faster than legacy 5V/2A USB-A — if the cable and phone support it.

Key speed concepts:

| Term | What it means | Summer note |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **Max PD watts** | Peak negotiated output (e.g., 18W, 30W, 65W) | Higher watts shorten top-up time but add heat — avoid charging in direct sun |
| **PPS (Programmable Power Supply)** | Fine-grained voltage steps for some Samsung/Google devices | Check phone compatibility; not every bank implements PPS |
| **Input vs output watts** | How fast the bank refills vs how fast it charges your phone | A 65W-out bank with 18W-in takes hours to refill overnight |
| **Pass-through** | Charge the bank and a phone from one wall adapter | Convenient in hotel rooms; quality varies by circuit design |

For outdoor festivals or beach days, **22.5W–30W PD** is a practical sweet spot for phones without laptop-class bulk. Laptop assist needs **45W–100W** class packs with e-marker USB-C cables — verify the manufacturer’s watt chart, not the box icon alone.

## Port types: what each connector still does in 2026

Most travel-ready packs combine **USB-C PD** with one or two **USB-A** ports. Here is how to prioritize:

- **USB-C PD (in/out)** — Default for 2026 phones, tablets, and many earbuds cases. Bidirectional ports let you recharge the bank with the same cable you use on the road.
- **USB-A (5V)** — Legacy for older accessories, small fans, or shared cables. Often capped at 12W–18W; fine for low-draw gear, slow for modern phones.
- **Wireless Qi pad** — Built into some “magnetic” packs. Convenient but less efficient; heat rises when the phone sits in a hot car or sunny bag.
- **Built-in cables** — Reduce forgotten-cable risk for kids on school break trips; frayed built-ins are harder to replace than a detachable USB-C lead.

If you already carry a [budget wireless earbuds](/en/blog/2026-budget-wireless-earbuds-top5) case and a [summer Bluetooth speaker](/en/blog/2026-summer-bluetooth-speakers-guide), count **simultaneous ports** — one PD port plus one USB-A often beats a single high-watt port when two devices need juice after an outdoor day.

## Safety certifications and when to walk away

Lithium-ion packs fail loudly when cells or protection circuits are cut-rate. Look for marks that indicate third-party safety testing, not just CE self-declaration stickers:

| Mark | Region / meaning | Why it matters |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **UL 2056 / UL listed** | U.S. safety standard for portable chargers | Common benchmark for North American retail |
| **CE** | EU conformity (manufacturer-declared) | Baseline in Europe; pair with known brands |
| **KC** | Korea Certification | Expected for official sale in Korea |
| **PSE** | Japan product safety | Relevant for Japan travel electronics |
| **UN38.3** | Lithium transport testing | Often required for air shipment compliance |

Red flags in public reviews: **swelling cases, sudden capacity drops after a few cycles, smell when charging, or tripping hotel outlets**. Prefer brands that publish cell supplier or protection IC details. Unknown no-name mega-mAh packs at impulse-checkout prices carry disproportionate risk — especially when left in a hot car during heat waves.

## FAQ

### Why does my phone charge slower from a power bank than from the wall?

The wall adapter may support higher PD/PPS profiles than the bank, or the cable lacks the correct e-marker for high current. Heat throttling on summer days also reduces charge speed — move the phone out of direct sunlight.

### Is a 20,000mAh power bank always better than 10,000mAh?

Not for pocket carry or flights. Larger packs add weight and heat. A 10,000mAh PD pack often covers one full phone cycle with less bulk; 20,000mAh suits multi-day trips if you accept ~74Wh and heavier bags.

### Can I take a power bank on an airplane?

Most airlines allow spare lithium batteries in **carry-on** baggage when each pack is **≤100Wh** (roughly 27,000mAh at 3.7V). Above 100Wh typically requires airline approval; many carriers disallow >160Wh. Check your carrier’s latest policy before {{today}} departures.

### What does 65W on the label actually mean?

It is the maximum negotiated USB-C PD output under ideal conditions — often split when two ports are active. It does not guarantee 65W to your phone unless the device, cable, and bank all support that profile.

### Do I need PPS for Samsung or Google phones?

PPS can improve fast-charge curves on supported models, but standard USB-C PD still charges them — just not at peak marketed watts. Read your phone’s supported profiles and match a bank that lists PPS if you want maximum speed.

### Are magnetic wireless power banks worth it?

They are convenient for short top-ups but lose efficiency and run hotter than wired PD. For all-day outdoor use, a wired USB-C connection is more predictable.
