## What BTU measures

**BTU per hour** states how much heat an air conditioner can remove from a space each hour. Higher BTU means more cooling capacity — but **oversizing** can short-cycle (cool quickly without dehumidifying), while **undersizing** runs non-stop in heat waves.

Think of BTU as **engine displacement** for cooling: enough to handle peak load, not so much that the unit never runs steadily.

## Baseline sizing table (moderate climate)

| Floor area | Typical BTU range | Notes |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 9–12 m² | 6,000–8,000 | Small bedroom |
| 12–18 m² | 8,000–10,000 | Standard bedroom |
| 18–23 m² | 10,000–12,000 | Large bedroom / small studio |
| 23–30 m² | 12,000–14,000 | Studio / living combo |
| 30+ m² | 14,000+ or multi-unit | Open plan |

Add **10–15% BTU** for west-facing windows, top-floor units, or poor insulation.

## Single-hose vs dual-hose and “effective BTU”

Portable AC labels use the same BTU numbers as window units, but **single-hose portables** pull conditioned air out and replace it with warm infiltration — effective cooling can feel **one size smaller**. Dual-hose models isolate intake and exhaust, preserving rated performance closer to window units.

## FAQ

### How many BTU for a 15 m² bedroom?

Start with **8,000 BTU** for a shaded room with 2.4 m ceilings. Bump to **9,000–10,000** if afternoon sun hits the window or insulation is poor.

### Does higher BTU always mean lower electricity bills?

No. Oversized units may cycle on/off frequently and struggle with humidity. Right-sizing for peak summer hours usually yields **better comfort per kWh**.

### Is 10,000 BTU enough for a 25 m² studio?

Often **borderline** for all-day cooling in humid summers. **12,000–14,000 BTU** or dual-hose portable is safer for open layouts.

### Do kitchen adjacency and ceiling height matter?

Yes. Kitchens add heat load; ceilings above 2.7 m increase volume. Multiply floor area by height when comparing guides — a 20 m² room with 3 m ceilings behaves like a larger space.

### Can I use two small units instead of one large one?

Yes in multi-room apartments — **zone cooling** can save energy if doors stay closed. One undersized unit for a whole studio is usually worse than one correctly sized unit.

### What about kW vs BTU on Korean labels?

Rough conversion: **1 kW cooling ≈ 3,412 BTU/h**. A 2.5 kW wall unit is about **8,500 BTU** class — always check the label’s BTU or kW line, not marketing names like “6평형.”

### Should renters choose lower BTU to save money upfront?

Cheaper small units cost less to buy but **run longer** in heat waves. Mid-size correct BTU often pays back in one summer via shorter run times.

## Key takeaways

1. Start with **floor area + ceiling height**, then add sun and insulation buffers.
2. **8,000 BTU** fits many 12–18 m² bedrooms; **14,000 BTU** class fits larger studios.
3. Portable **single-hose** units often need a **higher label BTU** than window units for the same room.
4. Oversizing hurts dehumidification; undersizing burns electricity all day.
5. When in doubt, size for **afternoon peak**, not spring mild days.
