## Introduction: budget laptops for students split on CPU, RAM, and outdoor readability

Last summer break my nephew called from his dorm mid-assignment — **five Chrome tabs in and the fan noise arrived first**. Mid-50s buyer lesson: sub-$550 laptops look identical in storefront grids, but without **16GB RAM, 250+ nits, and 45Wh battery** they fall apart fast in uncooled dorms and all-day library sessions.

Between summer projects and fall enrollment, **Ryzen 5 / Core i5** and 16GB defaults are common — yet **AC-free dorm heat**, **window-seat glare**, and **LMS + video + Slack at once** expose the gaps. Ryzen 3 / Core i3 choke on research tabs; **8GB with no SODIMM slot** locks you into cloud-only workflows; sub-250 nit panels wash out beside a sunny window.

Retail pages push **chassis color and “AI PC” stickers**, but the marketing lens I trust is four numbers: **CPU tier, RAM upgrade path, nits, and Wh**. A ~{{krw:499}} model can still split a four-year workhorse from swap hell depending on soldered 16GB vs 8GB plus a slot. Small tip: buy a RAM stick ($25–35) and external SSD during back-to-school sales so midterm upgrades do not wait on shipping.

This report ranks **five budget student laptops** by cross-checking public manufacturer specs, listed retail prices, and open reviews. We focus on **CPU class, RAM upgrade paths, display nits, and battery watt-hours** so readers match hardware to real campus scenarios—not retail ad copy.

## FAQ

### Is 8GB RAM still enough for a student laptop in 2026?

For light note-taking and one browser window, 8GB can survive—but **2026 coursework rarely stays light**. LMS tabs, Google Docs, Slack, and a video lecture simultaneously will push 8GB machines into swap, which feels sluggish and runs the fan harder in a hot dorm. The IdeaPad Slim 3 and Vivobook Go 15 ship with **16GB soldered**, which we treat as the safer default. If you buy 8GB, prioritize models with an **open SODIMM slot** (Acer Aspire 5, HP 15, Dell 3535) and budget for a matching 8GB stick before midterms.

### How many nits do I need if I study near a window or outside?

Indoor desk work often tolerates **250 nits**, but summer sun through a dorm window or a shaded patio can wash out the screen. Lenovo lists **300 nits** on the IdeaPad Slim 3—meaningful for readable PDFs without maxing brightness and draining battery. None of these budget panels match phone OLED brightness; for frequent outdoor use, pick the brightest spec you can afford and consider a matte anti-glare coating. Pair harsh-light sessions with a **portable fan or shaded spot**—heat and glare compound faster than specs suggest.

### Can I upgrade RAM later, or should I buy 16GB upfront?

**Upfront 16GB** saves hassle if RAM is soldered—as on the IdeaPad Slim 3 and Vivobook Go 15. If you choose Acer, HP, or Dell with a free slot, verify the manual for **single vs dual-channel** layout before buying a module. An 8GB + 8GB dual-channel kit often beats 16GB single-channel for integrated graphics in light editing. Summer back-to-school sales are a good window to add RAM and an external SSD together. Waiting until swap becomes unbearable mid-semester costs time you do not have.

## Five checks before you buy

1. **CPU tier** — Ryzen 5 / Core i5 minimum for tab-heavy research; Ryzen 3 / i3 save money but age faster.
2. **RAM path** — Prefer 16GB onboard; if 8GB, confirm an **accessible SODIMM slot** in the service manual.
3. **Display nits** — Target **≥250 nits**; 300 nits helps summer window glare without max brightness.
4. **Battery Wh** — **≥45Wh** for a realistic campus day; smaller packs need a PD power bank.
5. **Thermals in heat** — Read public reviews for fan noise under load; uncooled dorms expose weak cooling fast.
