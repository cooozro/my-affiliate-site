## Introduction: summer couch time when the windows stay shut

In July 2026, living rooms become indoor stadiums — baseball pennant races, Olympic replays, and late-night co-op sessions while outdoor heat pushes everyone behind closed blinds. The purchase mistake we see most often: chasing the thinnest OLED bezel without asking whether **afternoon glare on the panel** will wash out HDR highlights, or whether the **HDMI 2.1 port count** matches a PS5, Xbox Series X, and a soundbar at once.

Since 2023, mainstream 55–65 inch sets have converged on **4K 120 Hz panels**, **variable refresh rate (VRR)**, and **Auto Low Latency Mode (ALLM)** on at least two HDMI inputs. Cross-shopping still requires reading whether “HDMI 2.1” means full 48 Gbps on every port or only one, whether mini-LED local dimming zones beat OLED for **bright sports with score bugs in the corner**, and whether the smart TV platform still receives security updates three summers from now.

This report ranks **five widely stocked 2026-class televisions** by cross-checking public specs, listed USD prices, and open reviews. We focus on **verifiable brightness, reflection behavior, gaming port layout, and heat-season viewing workflows** — not proprietary commerce feeds.

**Key takeaway:** Best closed-room OLED value → LG C4; brightest QD-OLED gaming hub → Samsung S90D; mini-LED sports punch per dollar → TCL QM8; budget bright-room HDR → Hisense U8; reliable full-array LED with Sony processing → Sony X90L.

## 1. LG OLED C4 (OLED65C4)

**Strengths:** WOLED evo panel with four full-bandwidth HDMI 2.1 ports — rare flexibility for PS5, Xbox, and PC without a splitter; Dolby Vision Gaming supported on compatible titles; webOS 24 with thin bezel suits wall mounts in small apartments; near-instant pixel response eliminates motion blur on fast pitches; public reviews praise near-black uniformity for night baseball.

**Weaknesses:** Absolute peak brightness trails QD-OLED and mini-LED — score bugs and hockey ice can look dim if sunlight leaks around curtains; glossy screen shows lamp reflections when ceiling spots aim at the panel; OLED burn-in remains a theoretical risk for static news tickers — vary content during all-day CNN loops; 65-inch pricing climbs faster than 55-inch sweet spot.

**Analysis takeaway:** For most buyers, this translates to **the safest OLED all-rounder when you game on two consoles and stream Dolby Vision at night** — four HDMI 2.1 ports remove the cable-swapping headache that ruins a Sunday doubleheader.

**Verdict:** Best **OLED value and port layout** for mixed sports, streaming, and HDMI 2.1 gaming in a light-controlled room.

## 2. Samsung S90D (QN65S90D)

**Strengths:** QD-OLED delivers higher color volume and peak HDR punch than WOLED in Samsung’s published measurements; Gaming Hub aggregates cloud and local titles; four HDMI 2.1 inputs with 4K 120 Hz and AMD FreeSync Premium Pro; Object Tracking Sound+ on select sizes reduces need for a soundbar during quick MLB watch parties; Tizen app roster covers domestic Korean streamers and global services.

**Weaknesses:** Tizen ads in the home row annoy purists; no Dolby Vision support — HDR10+ and HDR10 only, which matters for some streaming catalogs; price premium over LG C4 for similar inch class; reflections still visible on QD-OLED glass when side windows face the panel.

**Analysis takeaway:** For most buyers, this translates to **the brightest OLED path when your living room stays dim but you want vivid jersey colors during World Cup replays** — accept HDR10+ ecosystem lock-in before checkout.

**Verdict:** Top **QD-OLED gaming and sports color** pick when brightness per dollar among OLEDs matters more than Dolby Vision.

**Strengths:** Mini-LED with high zone count (varies by size) pushes **about 2,000 nits** peak HDR per TCL spec — score bugs stay legible with curtains cracked for ventilation; 144 Hz panel headroom on PC; four HDMI 2.1 ports at 48 Gbps on 2024–2026 revisions; Google TV with broad app support; street pricing near {{krw:999}} undercuts OLED rivals by hundreds.

**Verdict:** Best **mini-LED value** when closed windows still leak daylight and sports HDR matters more than OLED black levels.

## FAQ

### Do I need HDMI 2.1 if I only watch streaming apps on the TV?

Built-in apps top out at 4K 60 Hz on most services — **HDMI 2.1 matters when a console or gaming PC sits in the chain**. If you plan a PS5, Xbox Series X, or a desktop GPU within the TV’s life, buy at least **two** full 48 Gbps HDMI 2.1 ports. Sports and movies from apps still benefit from HDR metadata and low-lag Game Mode when you occasionally plug in a laptop for a watch party.

### Is OLED safe for all-day sports channels with static score graphics?

Modern WOLED and QD-OLED panels include pixel shift and logo dimming, and burn-in risk dropped versus 2018 sets — but **static ticker bars for hundreds of hours** still stress emissive pixels. Mini-LED LCD avoids burn-in entirely. For mixed use — weekend games plus movies — OLED is fine. For a trading-desk TV running CNBC eight hours daily, pick mini-LED.

### Why do reviewers say “close the curtains” in summer if the TV is bright?

AC season often means **closed windows plus ceiling lights on** — reflections, not only sunlight, wash out OLED mid-tones. Mini-LED sets tolerate more ambient light. Before buying, note where lamps sit relative to the screen. A matte anti-glare LED can beat a glossy OLED in a bright Seoul living room even when blinds are shut.

### Should I wait for 2026 model-year badges or buy 2024–2025 stock on sale?

Mid-year clearance on **LG C4, Samsung S90D, and TCL QM8** often beats launch pricing on marginally brighter successors. Check HDMI 2.1 port count on the exact SKU — warehouse deals sometimes mix last year’s two-port boards. If the spec sheet lists four 48 Gbps ports and price is 20% below launch, heat-season shopping favors the discount.

## Five checks before you buy

1. **Count HDMI 2.1 devices** — PS5, Xbox, PC, and soundbar eARC each need a plan; two-port TVs force a switch or 4K 60 Hz compromise.
2. **Map window and lamp glare** — closed blinds plus a ceiling spot can ruin OLED more than mini-LED; sit where you watch before mounting.
3. **Confirm 48 Gbps on the ports you will use** — some sets label HDMI 2.1 but limit bandwidth on rear inputs 3–4.
4. **Match HDR format to your apps** — Dolby Vision (LG, Hisense, Sony) vs HDR10+ (Samsung) affects streaming catalog quality, not just gaming.
5. **Budget sound and heat** — summer AC plus a bright panel at high backlight wattage adds to the electric bill; a soundbar or ARC receiver often beats built-in tweeters for stadium mixes.
